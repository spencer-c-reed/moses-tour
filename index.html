The Bobs: Moses, Caro, and the Tour of New York

A self-guided one-day tour of Robert Moses's New York, drawn from Robert Caro's The Power Broker. All page references below are to The Power Broker (1974).
The tour runs Bronx → Manhattan → Randall's Island → Queens → Long Island, ending at Jones Beach at dusk. It's a long day — plan on 9 hours door-to-door. Two MTA legs, two rideshares, and transit back from Jones Beach (LIRR from Freeport, or the Jones Beach shuttle in summer). Wear comfortable shoes, bring water, sunscreen, and a towel/swimsuit for the final stop. Meals: bagels to kick off, lunch picked up on the way to Sheep Meadow, dinner at or near Jones Beach.

Stop 1 — Crotona Park / East Tremont (Start)

Location: Crotona Nature Center, 1215 E 173rd St, Bronx · Open in Google Maps

Time: 9:45–10:45am · bagels at the start, then walk through the park and along the Cross-Bronx Expressway to the Third Avenue bus depot site.

Significance. The opening chapter of the tour and of Caro's book: "Robert Moses built 627 miles of roads in and around New York City. This is the story of one of those miles." East Tremont was an integrated, working-class Jewish neighborhood with a deep civic fabric — destroyed, not by accident, but by Moses's refusal to shift the Cross-Bronx a single block north to spare 1,530 apartments. Look for the sudden swerve in the expressway: without it, 6 buildings would have come down; with it, 54 apartment buildings and 5,000 residents did.

Key pages: 213–221 (fight with the barons, eminent domain); 220 ("Nothing I have ever done has been tinged with legality"); 238 (Valley Stream expanding).

"Only recently you lost a substantial amount of... money in the Bronx" by "blocking the Bruckner Expressway"; the Cross-Bronx "will cost more than thirty million dollars additional. Would you like to see the project, now half completed, abandoned...?" — Moses to Borough President Lyons.
"By 1965, the community's very good, solid housing stock... were ravaged hulks. Windows, glassless except for the jagged edges around their frames, stared out on the street like sightless eyes." (on East Tremont five years after completion)

Tell the group: The alternate route along the park would have taken one obstacle — the Third Avenue Transit Company bus depot, quietly owned in part by Bronx politicians. "Whether Moses refused to change the route for a personal or political reason, the point is that his reason was the only one that counted."

→ Transit to Central Park (Stop 2): 174th St station (2/5 train) southbound to 59th St-Columbus Circle. ~40–45 min. Pick up lunch near Columbus Circle (Whole Foods, Zabar's, anywhere quick) — you'll eat it in Sheep Meadow.

Stop 2 — Lincoln Center (en route, optional walk-through)

Location: Lincoln Center Plaza, 10 Lincoln Center Plaza, Manhattan · Open in Google Maps

Time: 5–10 min walk-through on the way to Central Park.

Significance. By the 1960s, Moses had acquired vast Title I "slum clearance" powers. Lincoln Center is what he did with them: 18 square blocks razed, 7,000 low-income apartments destroyed, replaced by a cultural megaplex and 4,400 much more expensive housing units. Also a case study in the corruption that would eventually help bring him down — land sold to favored developers at $7/sq ft while others had offered $9.58, and 70 Columbus Ave (owned by the Kennedy family) purchased at $62.88/sq ft.

Key pages: 1015–1016 (Title I pressure campaign on HHFA).

"Moses could push a button and the calls would pour in... Calls from the people who really mattered." — Lutsky, federal Title I administrator.
→ Walk to Sheep Meadow: Enter Central Park at Columbus Circle, cut northeast to Sheep Meadow. ~15 min.

Stop 3 — Sheep Meadow, Central Park

Location: Sheep Meadow, Central Park · Open in Google Maps

Time: 11:45am–12:30pm. Lunch stop. Set the context of why people worshipped Moses at his peak.

Significance. "Moses didn't just fall out of a coconut tree." By 1932 Central Park was a ruin: potholed walks, benches on their backs, 9 of 10 Mall trees dead or dying, inbred deformed sheep, a "Hooverville" of 200 shacks in the drained reservoir bed. The Tammany-run Parks Department staffed itself with rheumy-eyed drunks who couldn't swim. Then, in one winter of 1934–35, Moses completed 1,700 of 1,800 planned park projects across the city. Sheep Meadow itself got the bulldozer-and-resurface treatment. This is the Moses people cheered for, and for good reason.

Key pages: 335–336 (pre-Moses parks); 369 (first day firings); 374 (winter of 1934–35 totals — 1,700 projects completed).

"Over the winter, 1,700 of 1,800 planned major parks projects and improvements had been completed... 7,000 dead trees removed, 11,000 new ones planted in their place... 22,500 benches reslatted, 62,000 others pruned." (374)
→ Walk east to Central Park Zoo: ~15 min across the park.

Stop 4 — Central Park Zoo / The Arsenal

Location: Central Park Zoo (Arsenal Building), 830 5th Ave, Manhattan · Open in Google Maps

Time: 12:45–1:00pm. Two things to talk about here: the Adventure Playground / "Battle of the Park" and the Zoo itself.

Significance – Adventure Playground (1956): Moses's first public defeat. He tried to bulldoze a shaded glen for a Tavern-on-the-Green parking lot. Forty mothers blocked the bulldozer with baby carriages; Moses sent men at 1:30am behind a wooden fence to clear-cut the trees anyway. Press coverage cracked the Moses mythos. The Adventure Playground stands here now on the site.

Significance – The Zoo: The whimsical side — Moses rebuilt the rotting Menagerie in 8 months as a gift to Al Smith, who lived across the street. Smith got a master key and "Honorary Night Superintendent" medal, and would walk over at night, feed Rosie the hippo apples, and growl "La Guardia!" at the big tiger to make it snarl for his Tammany friends.

Key pages: 1006 (Battle of the Park cascading into Title I investigation); earlier zoo/Smith material roughly 370–380.

"Oh, who are these critics anyway? Troublemakers. A small, noisy minority. You have Mr. Isaacs and the Citizens Union and these childless women howling about their non-existent children." — Moses, after returning from vacation mid-scandal. It did not land.
Sid Shapiro, a senior aide who idolized Moses: "It was such an insignificant thing... I don't know to this day what the hell those people got so excited about. But it was — I don't know — it was just never the same after that."
→ Transit to Randall's Island: Walk to 5th Ave/E 60th, take Q train or M60 SBS bus toward LaGuardia, or cab/rideshare over the Triborough. ~45–60 min. Goal: Triborough Bridge & Tunnel Authority HQ at the toll plaza.

Stop 5 — Randall's Island / Triborough Authority HQ

Location: Randall's Island Park, near the old Triborough (RFK) toll plaza · Open in Google Maps

Time: 1:45–2:45pm. This is the capital of the empire.

Significance. The Triborough Bridge itself is a wonder of engineering (1934–36) — four bridges plus 13,500 feet of viaducts tying three boroughs and two islands. But the authority Moses built around it is the real story: a state-within-a-state with its own flag, license plates, army (Bridge & Tunnel Officers), teletype network, and — most importantly — its own tolls-derived revenue stream, immune to city, state, and federal control because bond covenants are contracts protected by the U.S. Constitution. Moses's headquarters sat tucked just below the toll plaza, invisible to drivers, by design.

Key pages: 461–469 (reaching peak of power); 527 (Henry Hudson Bridge money mechanics); 623 (public authorities doctrine); 635 (no longer needed the mayor); 705–724, 729, 733, 735, 747 (power-broker positioning between federal, state, local, unions, old machine); 816, 826 (hospitality); 848 (tension between large public works and democracy).

"Here was a project to kindle the imagination. In size, its proportions were heroic... enough concrete to pave a four-lane highway from New York to Philadelphia, enough to reopen Depression-shuttered cement factories from Maine to the Mississippi."
"I think you had better read the agreements and contracts." — Moses to LaGuardia, after the Mayor objected that the Authority's bondholders had "absolutely nothing to say" about city administrative matters. LaGuardia read them. He was wrong.
"The broad powers possessed by the Board of Estimate... would normally have made ignoring it impossible. But many of those powers rested on the power of the purse — and the purse that was financing most of Moses' projects was not the Board's but the federal government's."
→ Rideshare to Flushing Meadows: ~25–45 min depending on traffic. ~$30–45. Destination: the Unisphere.

Stop 6 — Flushing Meadows Corona Park (The Unisphere)

Location: Unisphere, Flushing Meadows Corona Park, Queens · Open in Google Maps

Time: 3:30–4:30pm. Walk around the Unisphere, the old World's Fair grounds, and talk about the long arc of Moses's downfall.

Significance. Flushing Meadows was Moses's 40-year obsession — the "valley of ashes" from Gatsby, which he wanted to turn into the people's Central Park and name after himself. Two World's Fairs (1939, 1964) were vehicles for this dream. The 1964–65 Fair financially destroyed itself and what remained of Moses's public reputation. He publicly called reporters "jackals" and "hyenas," fired his closest friend George Spargo for delivering bad financial news, ended up paying bondholders 33 cents on the dollar, and committed the cardinal bankers' sin: lost them money. The park was never named after him.

Key pages: 787 (Impellitteri as puppet mayor); 848 (public works vs democracy); 878 (One-Mile political reasoning).

F. Scott Fitzgerald on pre-park Flushing Meadows: "a valley of ashes — a fantastic farm where ashes grow like wheat into ridges and hills and grotesque gardens, where ashes take the forms of houses and chimneys and rising smoke..."
"Rationally, logically, he knew that, for a public figure, fighting the media is a battle he can only lose... But it had long since ceased to be logic or reason that dictated his actions." (on Moses vs the press, 1964)
"The methods he had employed in building and running the World's Fair were not new. He had been using them for forty years. The only thing different was that this time the world had seen them."
→ Rideshare to Jones Beach: ~45–75 min down the Southern State Parkway (Moses's own road). ~$50–80. Destination: the central water tower, modeled on the Venice campanile.

Stop 7 — Jones Beach State Park (Finale)

Location: Jones Beach State Park, central water tower · Open in Google Maps

Time: 5:45–6:30pm tour; 6:30pm dinner (Boardwalk Restaurant or drive to a seafood spot in Freeport for the ride back).

Significance. The summer capital of the empire — and the place where Moses's legend earned itself in the first place. In 1921 a barren, windswept sand spit; by 1930 a bathing beach America had never seen, with bathhouses of Ohio sandstone and Barbizon brick (materials so expensive "simply not used on public buildings"), boat basins, archery ranges with medieval-bowman targets, water fountains operated by ship's wheels, diaper-changing stations (a U.S. first in any public or private building), and parkways from the city designed with artistry. But also: the first real demonstration of Moses's method — start it illegally, let public love of parks pressure the legislature into paying after the fact, cut the corrupt Nassau machine into the graft, and bulldoze the robber barons who thought their walls could stop him.

Key pages: 163 (sketching the canvas); 169 (the barons who didn't know Moses was staring at their walls); 184 (Macy/Taylor Estate seizure); 195, 201–202 (Smith's "Rabble? That's me" special session); 220 ("Nothing I have ever done has been tinged with legality"); 223–224 (bathhouse scale, materials); 816–826 (hospitality at Jones Beach).

"Rabble? That's me you're talking about." — Al Smith to Havemeyer when the North Shore baron complained about being overrun by "rabble from the city."
"Are you crazy?" — one of the famous architects Moses had hired, watching him sketch bathhouses and 10,000-car parking lots on the back of an envelope on a deserted sand bar with no road access.
"The important thing is to get things done." / "If the end doesn't justify the means, what does?" / "Once you did something physically, it was very hard for even a judge to undo it." / "Once you sink that first stake, they'll never make you pull it up." — Moses's lessons from Jones Beach that he would apply for the next forty years.

The closing question (for dinner): Wait until the evening to judge a man's legacy. What do we make of it?

→ Transit home: LIRR from Freeport (NICE N88 bus from Jones Beach field parking to Freeport LIRR, summer schedule) or the Jones Beach shuttle when running. Otherwise rideshare back to the city (~$120+ surge).
Logistical notes. Heavy walking day — comfortable shoes, sunscreen, water. Bring a bag with a towel, sunscreen, and swimsuit for Jones Beach. Food at Jones Beach is pricey; consider packing a snack for the evening. Costs are mostly transit: 2 MTA fares, 2 rideshares (Randall's → Flushing, Flushing → Jones Beach), and LIRR/rideshare back. Estimated total: $90–180 per person in transit depending on ride-splitting.

Reading prep. Even a quick skim of the Intro and Chapter 37 ("One Mile") of The Power Broker before the tour makes the East Tremont stop hit dramatically harder.
